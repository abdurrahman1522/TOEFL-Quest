World 3 · Academic Reading

Passage: The Origins of Coral Reefs

Read the passage, then answer the questions below.

⏱ 18:00

Coral reefs are among the most biologically diverse ecosystems on Earth, often compared to rainforests for the sheer density of species they support. Their formation, however, is a remarkably slow process that depends on a symbiotic relationship between coral polyps — small marine invertebrates — and microscopic algae called zooxanthellae.

The algae live within the tissue of the coral and, through photosynthesis, provide the polyp with the majority of its energy. In return, the coral offers the algae a protected environment and access to sunlight. This mutual dependence explains why reefs form almost exclusively in shallow, clear, sunlit waters, typically no deeper than 70 meters.

Over centuries, as generations of polyps secrete calcium carbonate skeletons and die, their remains accumulate into the vast limestone structures recognized today as reefs. A reef's growth rate rarely exceeds a few centimeters per year, meaning many of the world's largest reef systems have been forming continuously for thousands of years.

1. According to the passage, why do coral reefs typically form in shallow water?
A Shallow water contains more calcium carbonate.
B The algae living in coral tissue need sunlight to photosynthesize.
C Polyps cannot survive water pressure below 70 meters.
D Shallow water is warmer, which speeds skeletal growth.
Correct answer: B. The passage states the algae provide energy "through photosynthesis," which requires sunlight — explaining why reefs form in shallow, sunlit water rather than deep water.
2. The word "symbiotic" in paragraph 1 is closest in meaning to:
A Competitive
B Temporary
C Mutually beneficial
D Accidental
Correct answer: C. The passage describes each side providing something the other needs — a mutually beneficial, or symbiotic, relationship.
